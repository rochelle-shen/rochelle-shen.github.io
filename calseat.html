CalSeat aims to bridge the gap for students having a lack of information regarding study space availability and resources, which causes unneeded stress and frustration for those trying to get work done. CalSeat now presents the idea of having a web based application with the physical installation of a counter. This will accurately keep count of how many people are entering or leaving the study space, and the web app is able to live update the amount of current people at the preferred study space. Users are able to check the online status of the area without having to worry about spending wasted time wandering various study spots. I worked on this in a group of 4.

my roles included: Human-centered design, UI design, UX research

Goals

As a student, I find myself running into the problem everyday of trying to find study space on campus. Therefore, I found it easy to get immersed into the issue and begin digging into the research of trying to solve this common problem of: "How do we provide sufficient information to students regarding study space availability?"

Problem

UC Berkeley students lack information regarding on-campus study spaces.

Research

Quantitative

We conducted 15+ interviews and gathered survey data from over 70+ respondees. This is what we were able to assess:

  • There were 5 places on campus that over 90%+ of students were aware of. (Doe Library, Main Stacks Library, Moffitt Library, MLK student union, and Free Speech Cafe)
  • MLK student union and Moffitt Library were the top 2 places that students studied at.
  • Individuals study on campus because they get distracted at home
  • Students stay on campus for study for an average of 3-5 hours with a max of going to 2 study places.
  • People do not tend to find new study places even though the ones they go to are crowded all the time.
Qualitative

We observed the study habits of students at the common study spaces: MLK, Moffit, and Haas Library. These are some of the findings:

  • MLK & Moffitt were both very crowded and packed, whereas Haas library had multiple empty tables and seats.
  • Haas' study environment was generally more quiet, compared to Moffitt and MLK not because there were more people.
  • MLK and Moffitt seemed to be busier due to the location because the buildings were more centralized on campus.
  • Haas library seemed to be located further away on the edge of campus.
Secondary Research

We utilized as many other possible resources we could find to obtain better information about study spaces.

  • One Daily Cal article gave a glimpse on students' opinions of the popular spots on campus which allowed us to gain a better understanding of students' needs & wants.
  • The Berkeley library website is definitely an underutilized resource that offers students knowledge regarding the different types of study spaces that may best match their needs (e.g. noise levels, library hours).
  • Google search provides a graphic chart indicating the popular times when simply doing a search of the study space name.
Key Insights
From all this data, there are a few main points we drew upon to lead into the potential solution:
  • Students are unaware of the facilities that are offered at these study spaces including whiteboards, study group rooms, and computers.
  • People enjoy working alongside with friends, even if they are not studying the same thing.
  • Many study spaces do not always address the basic study needs: allowing food to be eaten, good temperature, availble bathrooms, and open outlets. Students feel that this greatly lowers their productivity.

Ideation

Questions to address
  • How might we track the number of seats available in study spaces?
  • How might we make information about study space availability more accessible and transparent?
  • How might we make study spaces less crowded on campus?
  • How might we utilize pre-existing spaces as an extension to the study spaces students use?
Brainwriting

This method focuses on ideating through coming up with potential scenarios that will solve the current problem. This involves all members of the parties to continue the story line by building it more in depth and detail until the last person finishes it up with some potential solution.

Starbursting

This method focuses on ideating through asking questions, which can be used iteratively to go deeper into understanding the product needs.

Idea Sketches of some potential ideas
  1. Study Live Camera app: allows users to easily see the preferred study space live on the app because of installed cameras at the study location
  2. Installing New Study Spaces: adding new study spaces can relieve the problem of overcrowding and also take into consideration the needs of students at study spaces that aren't accounted for
  3. Turnstile Web Tracker: having a physical tracker (e.g. a turnstile) that live counts amount of people entering and leaving, and these numbers and continually updated on an accompanying website
  4. Optimizing Current Space: people can not always use the outdoor space due to weather conditions so adding additional resources to the outdoor tables can allow students to take advantage of unused space already available

User flow: walkthrough of a student's daily process to get a better understanding at where the core problem begins and lies

Affinity Mapping: taking all the data we have obtained based on what users said about the seat issue and congregation the similar sticky notes into categories to break down the problem further

Solution

My top pick was the idea of combining a physical counter and having a web app together. These two fit like the perfect combination, since the counter provides a live update tracker and the website can display all needed information including the count, amenities, hours, and more. The student is able to just simply take 2 minutes to check online to see if their desired study space is available without taking 30+ minutes to walk to the spot and take a few rounds around the library.

Low-fi Concept Sketch

I kept the users' needs in mind and realized that the first step users wanted to do was just find their desired study space so having a huge search bar will help gravitate the user towards the search action. The users also have many specifications they preferred at each study location, so listing out the available resources would allow the user to see what works for them.


Med-fi Design

I turned the drawings into digital prototypes without focusing on the color choice. I realized that having a graph based on the current date will allow users to easily toggle to see the fluctuation between dates. This will also represent which times are more crowded without much cognitive effort and also let them see how many people are currently at the location.


Hi-fi Design

I flushed out the final color choices and ended up going with blue and yellow tones to match Berkeley's school colors (Go Bears!), since this application was designed with Berkeley students in mind. I wanted to also have a main landing page that provided a straightforward way to understand what the purpose of CalSeat was. I added icons next to the 'Resources available' area to make it easier for users to spot without getting mixed up in the clutter of words.

Final Thoughts

This project really stemmed out of a current issue I faced, so it meant a lot to me to know that there are feasible solutions. By better understanding other students' viewpoint on this situation, I came to realize that almost all people faced the lack of information about study space issue. Taking look back at the process and design, I feel like I had a very thorough user experience research side, but this limited me on the amount of time I got to spend on the user interface portion. There are a handful amount of changes I would like to implement and could easily critique my own product design right now. Nevertheless, I want to make this idea a reality and speak with our school ASUC senators to see if this can happen!
